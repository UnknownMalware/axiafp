What is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus?

There are actually two Fundamental Theorems of Calculus, both of which establish a relationship between functions, their derivatives, and their antiderivatives. The First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus states the relationship between the rate of change of the area under a function, (the integral), and the function itself.


\(\frac{d}{dx}\int _a^x f(t)dt = f(x)\)


Here we see that the rate of change of the area under a function is equal to the function. That makes sense, right?

The second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus gives us a way to evaluate definite integrals by establishing a relationship between the area under a function, and the value of the antiderivative at the endpoints.


\(\int _a^b f(x)dx = F(b)-F(a)\)


This equation tells us that the value of a definite integral is equal to the difference in the endpoints of the antiderivative


Proof of the First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Let's prove the First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, seen below:


\(\frac{d}{dx}\int _a^x f(t)dt = f(x)\)


First, we can rewrite the expression \(\frac{d}{dx}\int _a^x f(t)dt\) using the limit definition of a derivative


\(\lim\limits_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\int_a^{x+\Delta x} f(t)dt - \int_a^xf(t)dt}{\Delta x}\)


Using the properties of integrals, we can simplify the top expression, resulting in the following:


\(\lim\limits_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\int_x^{x+\Delta x} f(t) dt}{\Delta x}\)


Assuming that \(f(x)\) is continuous on \([x, x+\Delta x]\), by the MVT for Integrals we can say there exists a value c in that interval such that \(\int_x^{x+\Delta x} f(t) dt = f(c)\cdot \Delta x\). This means that we can rewrite our limit as the following:


\(\lim\limits_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{f(c)\cdot \Delta x}{\Delta x} = \lim\limits_{\Delta x\to 0}f(c)\)


Since c is between \(x\) and \(\Delta x\), \(\lim\limits_{\Delta x\to 0}x = x\), and \(\lim\limits_{\Delta x\to 0}x+\Delta x = x\)


By squeeze theorem, \(\lim\limits_{\Delta x\to 0}c = x\)


Therefore, \(\lim\limits_{\Delta x\to 0}f(c) = f(x)\), and we have shown that \(\frac{d}{dx}\int _a^x f(t)dt = f(x)\)
